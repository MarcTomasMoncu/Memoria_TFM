\section{Introduction}


Surgical site infections (Surgical Site Infections, SSI) constitute one of the most frequent complications within healthcare associated infections (Healthcare Associated Infections, HAI), contributing significantly to patient morbidity, to the increase in hospital stays and, consequently, to a rise in healthcare costs \cite{1_intro_morbidity}. From a clinical point of view, SSI are usually classified into superficial, deep and organ or space infections, depending on the level of tissue involvement, which determines both their severity and the difficulty in their identification and management.

The surveillance of HAI is recognized by the World Health Organization as a fundamental pillar for the improvement of infection prevention and control programs (IPC), facilitating the implementation of preventive practices and the evaluation of their impact \cite{2_intro_prevention}. In this sense, several studies have shown that participation in structured SSI surveillance programs is associated with a progressive reduction in the risk of developing these infections over time \cite{3_intro_decrease_risk}.

Traditionally, SSI surveillance in healthcare centers has been based on manual processes carried out by professionals specialized in IPC, who systematically review the clinical data of patients undergoing certain surgical interventions in order to determine the presence of infection \cite{4_intro_manual_diagnosis}. This approach, although considered the reference, is highly laborious, consumes a large amount of time and resources, and presents limitations in terms of scalability.

In the current context, characterized by the exponential increase in the availability of electronic clinical data, a growing interest has emerged in the transition towards automated or semi automated SSI surveillance systems \cite{5_automated_surveillance,13_prognostic_model_SSI,16_ai_models_review}. This evolution has led to multiple studies that explore the use of rule based algorithms and artificial intelligence techniques, especially machine learning models, for case identification \cite{16_ai_models_review,20_rule_based_models,21_semi_automated_surveillance}. Some of these approaches have shown good performance in terms of classification \cite{22_automated_detection_SSI,23_automated_detection_SSI,24_predictive_model_SSI}, although in many cases their external validity is limited \cite{25_intro_external_validation}. At the same time, semi automated strategies have been developed aimed at reducing the workload of professionals, focusing manual review on cases with higher risk \cite{20_rule_based_models,21_semi_automated_surveillance,26_intro_semi_automated_review}. These hybrid approaches appear as especially promising for their implementation in real clinical environments. However, the available evidence that directly compares the different methodological approaches continues to be limited in the specific context of SSI \cite{26_intro_semi_automated_review}.

In SSI surveillance, it is very important to differentiate between an approach oriented to retrospective detection and an approach that is centered on risk prediction. The first aim to locate infection cases once they have already developed, with an epidemiological or monitoring purpose \cite{intro_detection_SSI}. While the second seek to anticipate the probability of infection based on the variables available in the first phase of the care process \cite{intro_prediction_SSI}. This leads to important differences in terms of approach and decision making, ranging from the data and their nature to the clinical usefulness of the models.

If we move to a more methodological point of view, these approaches have advantages and disadvantages. For example, models based on rules present a high interpretability and transparency, facilitating their acceptance in clinical environments, but they have limitations in their capacity to find non linear relationships or complex interactions between variables\cite{20_rule_based_models}. On the other hand, machine learning models such as Random Forest allow modeling these relationships with much more flexibility and also robustness in front of noise present in the data\cite{12_machine_learning_SSI_prediction,15_intro_machine_learning}. If we go one step further, we find deep learning models such as dense neural networks, providing a very notable capacity in capturing highly complex and non linear patterns, although they require a larger volume of data and this increase in complexity also generates challenges in terms of interpretability \cite{intro_deep_neural_networks}. For this reason, it is very important to study this comparison between different approaches in order to reach a balance that allows optimal performance without losing clinical applicability\cite{16_ai_models_review, 20_rule_based_models}.

In coherence with this approach, this work uses three representative approaches: Quadratic Discriminant Analysis, as a classical statistical model that assumes specific distributions of the data and serves as a reference for interpretation and comparison between the models \cite{intro_Quadratic_Discriminant_Analysis}; Random Forest, as a robust ensemble method based on machine learning capable of capturing non linear relationships and complex interactions between variables \cite{intro_random_forest}; and dense neural networks, as a flexible approach with high potential to model complex patterns \cite{intro_dense_neural_networks}.

Trauma surgery constitutes a particularly relevant context for the study of SSI. This specialty focuses on the diagnosis and treatment of injuries of the musculoskeletal system, including bones, joints, ligaments, muscles and tendons. Due to the technical complexity of many of these interventions and the manipulation of deep tissues, these surgeries present a high susceptibility to infectious complications \cite{intro_epidemiology_fractures}.

In comparison with other surgical specialties, trauma surgery presents specific risk factors for the development of SSI. Among these, the frequent presence of implants or prostheses stands out, which can favor the formation of bacterial biofilms \cite{intro_implant_infections}. In addition, many interventions are performed in emergency contexts, with less control over optimal sterility conditions. Furthermore, patient related factors such as comorbidities or the severity of the initial injury can additionally contribute to infection risk. Overall, these elements make trauma surgery especially prone to SSI and justify the interest in developing specific models in this field \cite{intro_ssi_costs_orthotrauma,intro_ssi_risk_factors}.

Finally, the implementation of predictive models based on clinical data can raise a set of ethical and regulatory considerations. First, the quantity and quality of these data can introduce biases that affect the performance of the models across different patient subgroups, leading to implications in terms of equity \cite{intro_data_quality}. Second, the limited interpretability presented by some models may make acceptance by clinical professionals difficult and raises doubts regarding responsibility in decision making\cite{intro_dense_neural_networks}.

From a more regulatory perspective, these methodologies are framed within the category of software based medical devices. This implies compliance with validation and monitoring requirements, as well as external validation and evaluation in real environments being essential in order to guarantee the safety and effectiveness of the systems. At the same time, this integration should ensure a balance between human supervision and system automation, so that these systems act as support tools and do not replace critical thinking and professional judgment \cite{intro_ethics_ml}.

\section{Hypothesis and Objectives}

This work, when approached from an interpretative perspective, is not intended merely to identify or, in a certain sense, predict infections; rather, it aims to develop a decision-making framework structured across three principal levels: a probabilistic estimation, a clear definition of thresholds, and, ultimately, a meaningful impact on clinical practice. In this way, the following objective can be formulated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item To evaluate and compare the performance of different methodological approaches (namely classical statistical models, Machine Learning models, and Deep Learning models) focused on the retrospective surveillance of surgical site infections in trauma surgery, with the aim of determining the capacity of these models to support an efficient triage system while maintaining high sensitivity levels and thereby reducing the burden of manual review.
\end{itemize}

From this main objective, several specific objectives can be derived, corresponding to the different analytical components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item To compare the performance and development of predictive models (QDA, RF, and DNN) in terms of sensitivity, AUPRC, and other metrics considered relevant within the context of our data.
    \item To analyze and study the impact of decision-threshold selection in relation to the clinical behavior exhibited by the different models, attempting to maintain a high sensitivity threshold (=90\%) as a reference for clinical safety.
    \item To assess the potential reduction in workload for healthcare professionals enabled by the different model developments studied.
    \item To carry out a more focused comparison of advanced ML and DL models in order to identify real gains in effectiveness within clinical environments.
    \item To analyze the interpretability of these advanced models using methods such as SHAP, thereby establishing clinical coherence for the set of variables and their potential in healthcare settings.
    \item To explore the reliability and robustness of the models in light of common limitations in clinical data, such as imbalance, high dimensionality, or the risk of overfitting.
\end{itemize}

These objectives clearly orient the work toward a transition from purely technical evaluation to an analysis centered on future real-world clinical implementation. This leads to the formulation of the following general and specific hypotheses:

\begin{itemize}
    \item General hypothesis: Machine Learning and Deep Learning models offer improved performance in terms of overall discriminative capacity compared to classical statistical models (QDA), but require optimization of the decision threshold to achieve the sensitivity levels necessary in a clinical context.
    \item Specific hypothesis 1: Adjusting the classification threshold will allow ML and DL models to achieve sensitivity levels above 90%.
    \item Specific hypothesis 2: The QDA model will provide a better balance between interpretability and clinical performance, acting as an intermediate point between simpler and more complex models.
    \item Specific hypothesis 3: More complex models will be capable of capturing and representing complex interactions, but will exhibit vulnerability to overfitting in limited datasets.
    \item Specific hypothesis 4: The Random Forest model will demonstrate strong robustness to noise and outliers, but with a tendency toward metric optimization that may reduce sensitivity.
    \item Specific hypothesis 5: Rule-based models will be able to achieve very high sensitivity, but will be less effective in reducing workload.
    \item Specific hypothesis 6: The use of interpretability techniques such as SHAP will enable the visualization of clinical patterns, thereby enhancing trust in the outputs of more complex models.
\end{itemize}
